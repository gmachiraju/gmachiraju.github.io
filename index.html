Bio

I am a PhD Candidate at Stanford University's Department of Biomedical Data Science. My research interests are driven by biomedical applications and broadly span (1) the epirical study of application-driven theroretical questions to (2) the development of novel machine learning and computer vision methods to gain insights about tumor pathology and spatial biology at large. I am fortunate to be advised and mentored by Parag Mallick (Radiology). Much of my graduate training was graciously funded via the NIH (BD2K, NLM) with awarded additional scholarship from the Stanford Data Science Institute. Our lab's work is generously supported through various agencies including DARPA and NASA.

Before coming to Stanford for graduate training, I worked with Parag Mallick and Hermann Frieboes at the Canary Center at Stanford for Cancer Early Detection. Prior to that, I completed my B.A. in Applied Mathematics and a minor in Bioengineering at University of California, Berkeley. During my time at Cal, I was fortunate to work with Ian Holmes and multiple stellar scientists at Lawrence Berkeley National Lab (LBNL). Outside of research, I am passionate about teaching and mentoring and furthering diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) in academia.

As a side note: my name is most similarly pronounced as Batman's city of residence, "Gotham" (i.e. GAW-thum). There are many intonation variants for my Sanskrit-originating name across India, but this pronunciation is the closest to the North Indian variant and is the one I go by.

Research

Currently, my research aims to better our understanding of spatial systems and the key actors that distinguish such systems from one another. To this end, my thesis work seeks to extract human-interpretable, differentially expressed salient objects captured in high-resolution megapixel images. Rooted in the paradigm of weak supervision and motivation of model transparency, my methods and applications work both sit squarely in the eXplainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Interpretable Machine Learning (IML) research community. Some specific topics of interest are:

Weakly supervised Salient Object Detection (wsSOD)
Can image classifiers identify truly salient objects that are human-interpretable and differentially expressed? For deployment in human-centered applications, evaluation can't just be about accurate classification. Are some architectures inherently better at performing wsSOD and classification alike?
Model robustness under weak supervision
When predictive models are only given weak or coarse labels during training, how weak is too weak? Are some architectures more robust to weaker labels when performing classification and detecting salient objects?
Mitigating weak supervision in modeling
Maybe we can bypass the constraints of weak supervision to learn more holistic representations of images. We hypothesize that megapixel image classifiers that take spatial context into account should perform better at both wsSOD and classification. We are currently exploring Multi-task Auxiliary Learning and geostatitics-guided loss functions to incentivize spatial awareness and align AI explanation with human interpretation.

Primary applications seek to improve cancer prognostics by identifying histomorphological biomarkers of cancer progression in the tumor microenvironment. To do this, we analyze state-of-the-art hyperplexed immunofluorescence tissue microscopy images (>30 image channels) through amazing collaborations at GE Global Research and Stanford's Plevritis Lab. In such application and data domains, classifier interpretability is both necessary and enters a new frontier in explanation mapping due to the increased channel dimensionality of hyperplexed images.

Preprints & Publications

While my current research focuses on computer vision, I've been privileged to work in several informatics methods (and biomedical application) domains, including: graph representation learning (protein function prediction), ML for large-scale deployment (clinical decision support via the EHR, global health monitoring with satellite imagery), factorization methods (multi-omics & wearables data integration), statsitical inference (of cancer-associated gene enhancers), and mathematical and physics-based modeling (of tumor growth and protein shedding).
