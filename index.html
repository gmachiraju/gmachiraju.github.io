Bio

I am a PhD Candidate in Biomedical Data Science at Stanford University. My research interests broadly span (1) AI-human collaboration and (2) the discovery of salient objects within long-sequence data (e.g. large images, time-series, or graphs) to expand our understanding of science and biomedicine. I am fortunate to be mentored by Parag Mallick (Radiology) and Christopher Ré (Computer Science) and spend much of my time with the Hazy Research group at the Stanford AI Lab (SAIL). My graduate training has been graciously funded via the NIH (BD2K, NLM), the Stanford Data Science Institute (Data Science Scholarship), and the International Alliance for Cancer Early Detection (Canary-ACED Graduate Fellowship). Through my mentors, my work is also generously supported by various agencies and organizations including the DoD, ARL, DARPA, NASA, NIH, Google, and Stanford's Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence (HAI).

Before coming to Stanford for graduate training, I worked with Parag Mallick and Hermann Frieboes at the Canary Center at Stanford for Cancer Early Detection. Prior to that, I completed my B.A. in Applied Mathematics and a minor in Bioengineering at University of California, Berkeley. During my time at Cal, I was fortunate to work with Ian Holmes and multiple stellar scientists at Lawrence Berkeley National Lab (LBNL).

Other fun: On weekends, you can find me road-tripping to one of California's numerous regional, state, or national parks to spend time on the water and trails. My hobbies include running, cycling, hiking, camping, picnicking, hosting friends for dinner and cocktails, and cooking for crowds. I also love city trekking with friends and visiting cafés and roasteries, museums, used bookstores (with large maths sections), jazzy cocktail bars, and Irish pubs with live music sessions. Research

Methodologically, I currently work in the intersection of (A) deep learning architecture development, (B) relational reasoning over long-range dependencies and contexts (e.g. via weak supervision, self-supervision, multi-task learning), and (C) interpretability, alignment, and concept discovery. Motivated by scientific and biomedical applications, my thesis work focuses on large-scale computer vision in particular. One major focus of my thesis aims to better our understanding of spatial systems and the key actors that distinguish such systems from one another. To this end, we seek to extract human-interpretable, differentially expressed salient objects captured in high-resolution, gigapixel, and multiplexed/hyperspectral imagery. My thesis aims include:

Designing interpretability evaluation frameworks for architectural selection
For deployment in human-centered applications, evaluation of classifiers can't be anchored on accurate predictions alone. Are some architectures inherently better at detecting salient objects?
Designing inherently interpretable architectures & explanation methods
We reason that richer representations of data should construct more interpretable model explanations. For megapixel images, this may look like learning spatial context and long-range dependencies at multiple scales. Our architecture designs borrow ideas from deep State Space Models (SSMs), contrastive and self-supervised learning, and language modeling.
Applications to high-dimensional spatial biology for biomarker discovery

We seek to improve cancer prognostics by discovering histo-molecular biomarkers of cancer progression in the tumor microenvironment. To do this, we analyze state-of-the-art highly multiplexed immunofluorescence tissue microscopy images (>30 image channels) through amazing collaborations at GE Global Research and Stanford's Plevritis Lab. In such application and data domains, classifier interpretability is both necessary and enters a new frontier in explanation mapping due to the increased channel dimensionality of multiplexed images.
Research-adjecent interests: drawing connections between ML and classical ideas in maths/stats/physics, research design and communication as a CIRS Fellow at the Hasso Plattner Institute of Design, and mentoring and furthering DEI in academia through various non-profit organizations (including SMASH).

Preprints & Publications

While my current research focuses on long-sequence modeling in computer vision, I've been privileged to work in several methods and application domains, including: graph representation learning (for small molecule function prediction), ML for large-scale deployment (clinical decision support via the EHR, mobile health, global health monitoring via satellite imagery), factorization methods (multi-omics & wearables data integration), applied inference (to identify cancer-associated gene enhancers), and mathematical and physics-based modeling (of tumor growth and protein shedding).
